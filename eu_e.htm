The Maze Project as Integrated Project

our submission (yet)


Abstract:

The intensity of ground level cosmic radiation would be monitored in the number of detectors placed on secondary school buildings. Satellite base precise timing system would enable to study high energy cosmic ray events. Atmospheric and geomagnetic effects will be monitored and studied via short and long term cosmic ray intensity variation observed as registration of intensity of highly penetrating and ionising particles. High school students would be involved in studies. Data and results would be shared between schools using existing computer network. Information will be exchanged between arrays placed in different European towns and countries.

French physicist Prof. Roland Maze discovered (with P.Auger in 1938) extensive air showers, and, later, contributed to creation of Cosmic Ray Laboratory in Lodz.

NEED & RELEVANCE

The project is going to unify the pure scientific experiment with the digital age education on the secondary/high school level.

  • Educational aspects are addressed mainly to those students who wish to acquire knowledge and skills in the area of electronics, computing, physics and astrophysics, physics of the atmosphere to the level exceeding the standard school programs.
    • Experience shows that many students can easily cope with these problems,
    • even more, studying and solving such problems meet their interest and expectations.
    • Being involved in electronic/informatic problems would increase their ratio of creativity against consumption in this area.
    • Opportunity to be involved in the real scientific investigations would increase their educational interest.
  • The scientific target is in the poorly known area of cosmic ray physics and astrophysics. The special interest to investigate
    • practical problems to gain accurate time synchronisation to the 10 nanoseconds (corresponding to 3 meters) accuracy from satellite position/timing system
    • the Earth atmosphere influence on the ground level particle flux
    • geomagnetic disturbances causing air telecommunication problems
    • natural radiation level
    • very high energy cosmic ray phenomena (above laboratory available energies)
    • The highest energy cosmic rays

SCALE OF AMBITION & CRITICAL MASS

The Maze Project is addressed to young ambitious population and to scientists.

  • Within young population we expect following impacts:
    • Greater interest in the creativity than in consumption in the area of informatics, computing, electronics etc.
    • increase of skills for future employment,
    • turning more interest to role of science in development of communication, technology etc.,
    • Showing practically edges and limits for implementation of modern electronic/informatic technologies, which provides better start for planning future careers.
  • For scientific community (engaged with addressed fields) we expect
    • large area studies of the high altitude atmosphere (as pressure, density and temperature at high altitude correlate with secondary cosmic ray ground level counting rate),
    • short and long term variation of the high altitude atmosphere and atmospheric electric field,
    • correlation of cosmic ray intensity with climate changes and extreme weather phenomena,
    • more information about the geomagnetic phenomena, forecasts for radio transmission problems,
    • large number of very high energy cosmic ray events registered (for a large area covered by the arrays included in the Project),
    • more young power in the field

The scale of the experiments depends on the number of local areas starting up the project. We are considering mostly urban areas having minimum 4 secondary schools within 4km distance. As the information will be shared off-line via Internet the total gain is proportional to the total area covered whereas the accuracy of results is proportional the total area squared. The educational impact depends on the number of students involved.

INTEGRATION

Finding similarly oriented groups in other towns or countries would increase a demand for better communication with them (including visits, conferences etc.).

The basic detectors are described below. However, there is a wide area for innovation by adding or modifying equipment as well as for correlation of results with other available data (e.g. satellite measurements, weather influence etc.). Sharing this information (or/and possibly equipment) would unify young scientists from different areas.

Brief description of the idea of detectors

Secondary (high level) schools in the local area will be equipped with cosmic ray detectors.

One mini array unit:

We propose the array of 4 scintillation detectors of the area of 1m2 each. Detectors will be installed on the school roofs at the corners of square and separated by about 10-30m. The light from the counter will be collected by one photo-multiplier per detector and the signal will be digitalized at the local on-line control system. The system will be equipped with the accurate absolute time clock (with relative accuracy at the local area near to 10 nanosecond) corrected by the satellite positioning system (probably GPS at the beginning).

Method of data collection:

The detector would be working permanently. It would require human activity for data transmission only (in the case of correct work). The on-line system can accumulate data for a time of the order of 5 weeks without data transmission.

Communication between mini arrays:

All mini arrays from the local area will transmit the data off-line using urban internet lines to one central system. The data would be accessible at the mini array level. However the standard access would be provided from the central system via internet connection. Every participant would have access to the central system data.

Methods of data processing:

Any participant could use his software or use available software created by other co-participants. Generally PC-based Linux with C++ (probably CERN's ROOT subroutines) will be preferred (open systems are generally more creative oriented). Results would be available on the individual (personal, school) web pages. Scientifically important results would be present also on the local official web page.

Brief description of expected events and related targets:

  • At the separate detector level (4 separate pieces of information per mini array) the secondary cosmic ray particle flux can be constantly measured. The intensity is about 170 counts per second for 1m2 detector (corresponding to 6 penetrating and ionising particles passing per second per human head or 1 particle per hand).
    • The intensity is atmospheric pressure dependent (see physical background) which gives a few percent variations (can be easily corrected).
    • geomagnetic disturbances can be seen as 2-4 percent variation, most of these follow solar activity and coronal mass emission towards the Earth, and have bad influence on the radio wave transmission, satellite behaviour etc.
    • small (however short time) variation would reflect high altitude atmospheric temperature changing,
    • thunderstorms and other atmospheric violent effects can alter cosmic ray muons counting rate via the change of the Earth electric field (poorly known effects, seen already in a few experiments sometimes as disturbance, e.g. L3+Cosmics at CERN),
    • long term variation due to 11 year solar cycle, probably correlated with global Earth weather changing,
    • test of apparatus (similar behaviour of all detectors),
    • educational example of the mathematical fluctuations and statistical distributions

Data will be accumulated from all local mini arrays strengthening the effects.
  • At the separate mini array level (4 detectors) the showers of secondary particles (mainly electromagnetic: photons, electrons and positrons) will be detected with the rate about 1 per minute. Precise measurements of time difference between pulses from 4 detectors (with accuracy of the order of 1 nanosecond) would enable one to determine the direction of the primary very energetic cosmic ray particle. The amplitudes (digitalized) of signals provide information about energy of the primary cosmic ray particle.
    • such events have energies above 1015 eV, very important energy region to study the origin of cosmic rays,
    • theoretical problem of methods of accelerating particles in nature (as the most powerful human made accelerators cannot yet get energies of 1013eV),
    • small number statistics problems can be learned practically.
  • The off-line correlation of arriving time from events registered at the local area would enable one to find events passing through many mini arrays within the same 400 nanoseconds. The precise (about 10 nanoseconds) absolute time records give opportunity to determine the direction of primary particle. The energy of primary particle in such events typically exceeds 1018eV. For mini arrays covering area of 100 km2 (10km x 10km) it would happen about once a day (400 per year).
    • a cluster of about 30 mini arrays has an area comparable with the largest existing detectors for highest energy cosmic rays, therefore quite a few such clusters would provide scientific results of the highest value,
    • Achievement, monitoring and using the time system providing absolute time accuracy of 10 nanoseconds (3 meters in space) is very important for future navigation systems.

Technical details of mini-arrays

The estimated material cost of one mini array (one school) is about EUR 5.000,-.
This include 42 kg of polystyrene based scintillator (MINOS experiment type) 4 photomultipliers (small diameter), re-emitting fibres, GPS receiver and antenna, PC elements for on-line systems, housings, covers, cables and electronics (most costly are scintillator and phototubes).
The single 1m2 scintillation counter would be made of 1cm thick polystyrene base scintillation material. Via re-emitting fibres the light is transmitted to photomultiplier (one per 1m2 counter). Signals are transmitted to the on line computer. For signals arriving simultaneously (20 nanoseconds) from min. 2 counters the accurate time is recorded together with the signal amplitude information. The counting rate of the individual 1m2 detector is recorded every 5 minutes.

The system collects the data permanently. It gains about 150kB per hour or 3.5 MB per day.

Some physical background for cosmic radiation

Cosmic rays permanently bombard Earth coming mostly from outside the Solar System. Solar wind can influence penetration of the inner part of the Solar System (where we are) for relatively low energy cosmic ray particles. Therefore their fluxes at the Earth vicinity depend on the solar activity.
Most of cosmic rays are charged, ionised and stable particle (including nuclei). To get to the top of atmosphere the have to go across the Earth magnetic field, which pushes away low energy particles (so called geomagnetic cut-off, it is latitude dependent). Therefore disturbance of the Earth magnetic field alter the ground level cosmic ray intensity registration.
We are sheltered from cosmic ray bombarding by the atmosphere. Energetic particles interact with nuclei of atmosphere and produce secondaries or even cascades of secondary particles. Results of these processes can be observed as the secondary cosmic rays at the ground level. The observer altitude and the state of the high altitude atmosphere is very important for observed events. Some secondary particles can decay in the low-density high altitude atmosphere to highly penetrating and ionising particles called muons. These form the main contribution to natural radioactivity due to cosmic rays. Approximately 6 muons are passing through our heads every second. Very high energy cosmic ray particles produce cascades of secondaries which can reach the ground level. The cascades consist mainly of electromagnetic component (photons, electrons and positrons - anti-electrons). Their densities can exceed locally thousands per meter squared (per event).

Some historical background for cosmic rays

The phenomenon of cosmic radiation is known for 90 years. It was discovered (as a Hoehenstrahlung) by German physicist Victor Hess (Nobel Prize in 1936) and now we know that the Earth is constantly bombarded by the high energy particles coming from the outer space. High-energy particles form in the atmosphere so-called Extensive Air Showers (EAS) which were discovered by Roland Maze and Pierre Auger in 1938.
The first EAS array was built by Maze on the roof of Ècole Normale Supèrieure in Paris.
At the beginning of the fifties, Roland Maze closely collaborated with A. Zawadzki and other Polish physicists creating the comic ray laboratory in Lodz.
Cosmic ray studies are recently intensively developed, especially in the region of the upper limit of energy spectrum, where several single cases of particles with energies exceeding 1020eV (about 50 J) have been observed.
